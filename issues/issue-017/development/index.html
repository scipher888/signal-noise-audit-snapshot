Session 2 - Reliance, rescue, and the first moral ledger

The author · June 24 - reliance problem
I think the reliance problem is important. If a person, community, NGO, or country builds their life-sustaining system around a relied-upon source of aid, then suddenly removing that aid may be materially different than never having provided it. But that does not automatically make one person a murderer. It may create responsibility, or a duty to transition, or a duty to warn. It also exposes the hypocrisy problem: if cutting off aid means killing, then why are the capable non-rescuers who never stepped in treated as morally invisible? I also think the Singer trap matters. If every preventable death becomes a murder accusation against whoever could have helped, then almost everyone with resources becomes implicated. And if that is too much, then the accusation needs more discipline.
AI editorial process · June 24 - better moral category
My read: this is stronger now. The essay is not "Musk is innocent" or "USAID is bad." It is: we need a moral category between "nothing happened" and "you killed children." The reliance question gives the essay teeth. Withdrawal after dependence may create a different obligation than never having acted. But the accusation still needs a chain: who created reliance, who had authority, what warning or transition was owed, what harm occurred, and what alternatives existed.
AI editorial process · June 24 - candidate line
The question is not whether the person who drops a lifeline bears responsibility. It is why everyone capable of picking it up disappears from the moral ledger.
The author · June 24 - capable non-rescuers
I think if 10 people are standing around and watching someone die, they are all essentially 100% responsible in some way. It does not make sense to say the person who stopped helping is 100% responsible and the capable non-rescuers are 0% responsible. Capable non-rescuers are not less responsible to address it and therefore they are not clear of the murder accusations they are making.
AI editorial process · June 24 - rescue-ledger refinement
The draft route shifted from pure reliance to a rescue-ledger distinction: withdrawing a relied-upon lifeline can be morally serious, but the accusation cannot make every other capable actor disappear. The process kept separating emergency repair, responsibility, and murder language so the essay would not collapse those categories.
AI editorial process · June 24 - recurring line family
The early line family became: drops, rescue, ledgers. A relied-upon lifeline matters. So do capable bystanders. If the moral category is "you killed children because you did not keep funding rescue," the ledger cannot stop wherever the accusation finds its preferred villain.
